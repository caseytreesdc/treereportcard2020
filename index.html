Welcome

Casey Trees is a Washington, D.C. - based nonprofit committed to restoring, enhancing and protecting the tree canopy of our nation’s capital. We pursue our mission through community action, education and research.

Casey Trees’ Tree Report Card measures the quantity and condition of D.C.’s trees and the collective efforts of all groups and individuals working to achieve the District’s 40 percent tree canopy goal. It is based on data collected from both public and private sources, including the District and Federal Governments, universities, developers, nonprofits and others.

Summary

Executive Summary

Spring is a time for new growth, but before we look forward toward reaching our 40 percent tree canopy goal, we must look back. The Casey Trees annual Tree Report Card reviews and highlights the successes of our previous year and calls attention to where we can do more. I am happy to report that our tree canopy has continued to improve; we exceeded our tree planting goals and a majority of the trees in the District are still in good or excellent health. However, many of our trees are still at risk for removal and we continue to see impervious surfaces dominate our landscape.

The theme for this year’s Tree Report Card is Empowering Future Generations Through Trees. To grow a greater, greener city, we must work together, not just today or tomorrow, but day after day and year after year. When we teach younger generations about the value of trees and green space now, we will ensure that our city’s lush tree filled legacy continues.

I want to thank everyone who has been involved in restoring, enhancing and protecting the District’s tree canopy over the past year. It is through your involvement, donations and hard work that we are, and will continue to be, the “City of Trees”.

Thanks as always for your support!

Spotlight

Background

Connecting with the environment provides all of us, but particularly youngsters, with many benefits – education, physical activity, social connections – the list is endless. And especially in the urban environment where recreational space is at a premium, trees can create these spaces to experience this nature – all the while creating lifelong connections to it.

Trees are one of our city’s most valuable resources, and their existence depends on our care. It can take generations for a tree to grow to maturity, but only a moment for it to be cut down. And when a large tree is removed, it doesn’t just negatively impact the property where it was located, but rather the entire neighborhood. To successfully protect the city’s trees from illegal removal, we must make sure that younger generations interact with and experience them in a way that provides them an appreciation of their aesthetic, social and health benefits first-hand.

Empower to be Healthy

Playgrounds are a place for kids to experience their local environment through outdoor activities. Unfortunately, on a hot summer day, the urban heat island effect makes plastic and concrete play spaces extremely hot and often unusable. Maintaining trees and green spaces around play structures creates a cool and welcoming environment that kids are comfortable playing in.

Outdoor play spaces promote physical activity, with many well documented public health benefits: a decrease in obesity and varied chronic illnesses, improved spatial awareness and sensory development, and improved immune systems. When we empower kids to go outside and play, we are promoting their overall health, all the while developing their appreciation of the natural world that we all must learn to protect and care for.

Empower to Grow

Outdoor spaces don’t just promote physical health – just important is how they help the development of social skills such as communication, listening and conflict-resolution. When we empower kids to understand their role in a community of shared space which is what our urban environment is, we are also empowering them to work together, respect and protect that space, and the people who call it home.

Empower to Lead

When children are taken outdoors, they are given a chance to explore the natural world and learn about the value of their local environment. Environmental education is an important part of a child’s education and kids who are given the opportunity to go outside and have hands on learning experiences, whether it is with a class or with a friend, in a city or a small town, are more likely to be self-motivated, creative and critical thinkers and confident in their abilities. Together, these skills create strong leaders. When we empower kids to go outside and learn, we are also teaching them the skills necessary to become well rounded future leaders that will understand the value of their environment.

Ways to Engage Youth

1

Go outside! You don’t need to create a formal teaching environment to learn. Taking walks around areas with high tree canopy or going to a park exposes kids to their local environment and opens a platform for them to ask questions.

2

Advocate for outdoor education programs! Learning about the environment is one thing, but experiencing it is another. Support outdoor learning in all D.C. public schools and encourage adding more trees and green spaces around playgrounds and parks so all students educated in D.C. can have access to nature.

3

Volunteer with Casey Trees! Learn how to advocate, volunteer to plant trees in and around parks or plant a tree on your yard with our Riversmart Homes program.

4

Donate to help students of all ages experience learn about the value of trees.

5

Get involved in local environmental programming in the District. Try your hand at gardening, urban farming, bee keeping, and more. Residents of all ages are welcome.

Metrics Explained

   

The Tree Report Card rates Washington, D.C.’s urban forest based on four metrics: Tree Coverage, Tree Health, Tree Planting and Tree Protection. Each one of these metrics is evaluated and given a letter grade. The overall grade for the District’s tree canopy is the average of these scores. The average grade for the District’s tree canopy is an A-.

Tree Coverage

This metric measures tree crowns or canopy when looked at from an aerial view. Every five years, Casey Trees uses satellite imaging to measure the growth of the District’s tree canopy. Tracking tree coverage ensures that we remain on track to reach our 40 percent tree canopy goal by 2032. When last measured in 2016, the District had 38 percent tree canopy coverage. For this reason, Tree Coverage receives an A grade.

Tree Health

This metric evaluates the overall condition of D.C.’s trees based on a sample taken from across the city. Measurements include tree size, species and condition, which are then examined to determine how resilient that canopy can be when faced with various pests and diseases.

The latest sample results showed little change in tree health from 2010’s data, with most trees being in good to excellent health. Tree Health therefore receives a B- grade.

0%

Calculations Explained

Trees in good to excellent health/Trees surveyed

166/201 = 0.83 or 83%

Tree Planting

This metric compares the number of trees planted each year to the number required for the city to meet it’s 40% canopy goal. In 2019, there were 13,748 trees planted throughout the District, or 3,100 over the target of 10,648. We have surpassed this number for the last 5 years. For this reason, Tree Planting for 2019 receives an A+ grade.

Calculations Explained

Total trees planted/tree planting goal

Tree Protection

Unlike the tree coverage, health and planting metrics, the Tree Protection metric evaluates the success of the city's tree laws in protecting trees. The grade for tree protection is measured by averaging the score of submetrics.

We use three submetrics to assess the implementation and the enforcement of the 2002 Urban Forest Preservation Act and the 2016 Tree Canopy Protection Amendment Act, which created Special and Heritage trees.

These laws provide protection for mature trees at risk of being removed by requiring people or nongovernmental entities to obtain permits if they wish to remove Special trees, making it illegal to remove Heritage trees and creating fines for noncompliance. Mature trees provide the most benefits to the District and without successful tree protection policies, these trees are at constant risk for removal. Fee Adjustment: Are the fees and fines levied keeping pace with inflation?
Using the Consumer Price Index, we can see that inflation from 2016 to 2019 increased the Special Tree Removal Permit fee from $55 per inch circumference to $59.66. Per inch, this is not a significant difference. However, increasing this fee to meet inflation would have resulted in $140,779.58 more being collected. This submetric meets the threshold of an A-.

Fee Management: Are fees and fines being used to plant replacement trees?
The Urban Forest Preservation Act states that the money collected from the Special Tree Removal Permit fees and illegal tree removal fines must be used to plant trees on public and private land. This money continues to be used for this purpose. Therefore, we are giving this submetric an A+.

Tree Survival: Are the replacement trees surviving?
Tree survival data shows us that two out of every ten trees planted in an urban setting grow to Special tree size. The amount of money that is received for every Special tree removed is enough to plant 10 new trees. At least 2 of those ten trees planted will survive and grow to become mature special tree sized. Therefore, we are giving this submetric an A-.

Heritage Tree Protection: is the law prohibiting removal of Heritage Trees being honored?
Eight heritage trees were cut down at the Sursum Corda development in 2019, resulting in significant canopy loss to a neighborhood already suffering from low tree canopy and dangerously high summer temperatures. The city allowed the developer to remove these trees reasoning that the development was undergoing zoning review prior to the law’s passage. However this reasoning is very unusual, if not unprecedented. Even the city’s own agencies including DDOT and DOEE stood firm behind preserving the trees, and for this we applaud them, but the result unfortunately was the same. Given the circumstances surrounding this case, we will withhold a grade for this submetric. Next year we will reconsider a new way to measure this provision of the law to add greater clarity.

The average of these four submetrics results in an A- for Tree Protection.

Recommendations

1

Protecting open spaces from being developed by working with the D.C. Council, District agencies and stakeholders to identify city-owned greenspace that should be conserved forever.

2

Expanding the Urban Forest Preservation Act to protect all trees of Special and Heritage tree size, no matter if they are on public or private land.

3

Updating the Environmental Literacy Plan and ensure all children throughout the District are receiving the necessary environmental education both in-classroom and outdoors in order to ensure the long-term stewardship of the canopy.

4

Adopting a city-wide impervious surface maximum percentage to protect open spaces from being developed and ensure a lush city for years to come.
